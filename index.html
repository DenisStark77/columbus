The Place

Not a development. Not a condominio. Something else.

Columbus Heights was planned as a 200+ lot development a decade and a half ago. The developer is no longer here. What remains is the land, the infrastructure that was built, and the people who chose to actually live on it. Five or six families full-time. Ten or twelve houses standing. A lot of vacant lots — some owned by speculators, some quietly waiting for the right neighbor to find them.

What's here

A gated entrance and eight kilometers of paved roads (genuinely rare in rural Costa Rica — one of the things we love most about this place), maintained by a volunteer HOA. Community water with reliable pressure. Starlink internet that runs our remote work life day to day. A handful of permanent neighbors who know each other and look out for one another. And then there are the other residents — deer, wild boars, macaws, toucans, dozens of other birds, the hills, the river, the canopy. Ocean breezes and an exceptional microclimate.

What's not

No developer to call. No grid electricity — the national utility has an unresolved dispute with the original project, so every house here runs on solar (which works beautifully). The HOA is volunteer; not every absent owner pays dues. No clubhouse, no manicured amenities, no rules about the color of your roof. If that sounds like a problem to you, this is probably not your place. If it sounds like freedom, keep reading.

How We Live

A small life, well-built.

We've been here five years. We built our home with our own choices. We grow some of what we eat. We bake our own bread. We pay $0 a month for electricity. None of this is exotic — it's just what becomes possible when nobody is telling you what to do.

If You Own a Lot Here

For fellow owners.

Whether you're caring for a parcel from far away, getting ready to build, or thinking about finding the right next neighbor to take it on — here's how we help. Different paths, same intent: keep this place healthy and in good hands.

Selling

Find the right next owner

Every potential buyer who comes through us has been through our orientation questionnaire and a real day on the ground — people who want to actually live here, not flip the lot. When the right person and the right parcel meet, we facilitate the introduction. Modest finder's fee on closing, far less than a traditional agent.

Maintenance

Eyes on your lot

Monthly brush cutting, perimeter check, photo report, and a heads-up if anything needs attention. Coordinated by a long-time neighbor who knows the terrain and the seasons. Simple monthly subscription per lot — peace of mind from anywhere in the world.

Improvement

Plant a fruit forest

Turn your lot into a productive food forest in your absence — local species, sensibly planned, planted from our own seedlings and tended through the first year. The lot you eventually arrive at has shade, fruit, and ten years less waiting.

Building

When it's time to arrive

Ready to build or move in? Lawyer and immigration referrals, builder and solar installer introductions, water system planning, sourcing materials locally, the first hundred small questions of life in Costa Rica. So your first months are about the place, not the paperwork.

Before You Book

Common questions.

Do I have to be planning to buy a lot to book a day?
Not at all. Some of our favorite visits have been with people who were just curious about off-grid living in the tropics. The day stands on its own.
Why is there a fee for the orientation call?
We're not real estate agents and we don't earn commissions on lots. Over the years we've done hundreds of free conversations, which was lovely but unsustainable. The $50 orientation covers our time and quietly filters for people serious enough to invest something in finding out. If you continue with us — to a longer call or to an in-person visit — the $50 credits toward that.
What about schools for our kids?
Three options at increasing distances. The public school in Quebrada Amarilla is a three-minute drive, Spanish-only — a real immersion path for kids open to it. Playa Hermosa, seven minutes away, has several schools including bilingual options (Spanish and English). For an English-first or international curriculum, Journey School in Jacó is an IB school running English, Spanish, and all grade levels. Several families also homeschool — we can talk through what that looks like during your day.
Healthcare — what's nearby?
Jacó is about fifteen minutes from the gate — several hospitals, multiple dentists, and a handful of alternative-health practitioners. The basics and the not-so-basics are both within easy reach.
How's the internet? Can we work remotely?
Starlink works exceptionally well here. We run video calls, design work, and full remote-work setups on it without issue. Cell coverage is solid as a backup. Several neighbors here work remotely full-time in tech and creative fields.
How do I actually get there?
We'll send full directions once your visit is confirmed. A regular rental car is fine — the road from the highway to the gate is concrete, and the roads inside the community are paved. No 4x4 required. We can also recommend a local driver if you'd rather not drive.
What should I bring?
Walking shoes, sun protection, a water bottle, and an appetite for honest conversation. The terrain is hilly but not difficult.
Is it kid-friendly?
Yes — children are welcome and the garden is endlessly interesting. Let us know ages in your message so we can adjust the walk.
What about rain?
Costa Rican rain is part of the experience — and most of it falls in predictable afternoon windows. We'll adjust the day around the weather. We don't reschedule unless conditions are genuinely unsafe.
What languages?
We host in English and Russian. Conversational Spanish is fine too.
